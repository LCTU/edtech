Preparation

The sections below are intended to help prepare for the reading of the text. The Background section has some basic information about the text. The next section suggests a number of questions that you might ask yourself before you start reading, trying to predict what the text might contain. The New Words section contains a list of key words that appear in the text while the Reading Strategies section introduces a range of different reading strategies.

Background

The text talks about auspicious pattern in Chinese culture, specifically the lions, seen outside buildings and the three rams representing the arrival of something new and auspicious.

You can find further information on auspicious images here and here.

Preparatory Questions

Asking yourself a number of questions to predict what you are likely to encounter when reading the text can help with your understanding. Here are some simple questions to start with:

What is the text about?

Expect words like image (形象) form (形式), tradition (传统), happiness (幸福), or good luck (好运). etc.

What period of history does the text cover?

Look for words like history (史), dynasty (朝) or the name of historical epochs e.g. the Han, Tang, Song, Ming and Qing dynasties (汉, 唐, 宋, 明, 清).

Where do you find such patterns?

Look for words for locations like temple (寺庙), palace (宫殿), park (园) and so on.

Who used the patterns and for what purpose?

Look for names, social groups, e.g. emperor (皇帝), Buddha (佛).

Reading Strategies

Below are a couple of suggestion that might help you improve your reading skills.

  • Think about the context of the text. Having read the title what do you expect the text to be about? Does the type of publication suggest something about the content or style you are likely to encounter?
  • What words and structures are you likely to meet? What are the likely key words and how might logical connections or temporal sequences be expressed?
  • How might the text be structured? Can you identify an introduction, main body and conclusion?
  • If you have access to an audio recording can listening to the recording help you identify word and phrasal boundaries?
  • Explore different reading strategies. Try listening for gist and establish what the text is about. Use the wh-questions to guide you. What, who, where, when and how?
  • Ask yourself a specific question and try to find the answer in the text quickly. For example, in what year was the Confucius Institute established, or where does it have offices?
  • Finally read the text in detail and make sure you understand what it says.
  • For each sentence analyse how it breaks down into phrases and how these are linked. What is the core sentences and how are the core elements elaborated.
  • In complex sentences look for possible topic comment structures.
